Gas constant

Whenever we do stoichiometry calculations involving gasses at STP, one can simply convert between moles and liters using $22.4$ as a "magic factor".
$1.00\ \mathrm{mol\ H_2} \cdot \frac{22.4\ \mathrm{L}}{1\ \mathrm{mol}}=22.4\ \mathrm{L\ H_2}$
Why does this work? Well, this constant is derived from the ideal gas law, discovered by Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron. The equation is $PV=nRT$ where P is pressure, V is volume, n is number of moles, T is the temperature, and R is a constant depending on the units. In the case of $\mathrm{\frac{L·atm}{mol·K}}$, the value for R is ${0.08205}$ For STP, which is 1 atm and 273 K, we get $\frac{(0.08205\ \frac{\mathrm{L}\cdot\mathrm{atm}}{\mathrm{mol}\cdot\mathrm{K}} \cdot 273\ \mathrm{K})}{1\ \mathrm{atm}} = \frac{22.4\ \mathrm{L}}{1\ \mathrm{mol}}$ For other temperatures and pressures, we need to derive a new conversion factor using the ideal gas law. Note that this ideal gas law takes a few assumptions into account.
